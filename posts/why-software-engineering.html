Why I Chose Software Engineering

January 1, 2026 5 min read By Anjana Wijerathna

Choosing a career path is scary. Here's the story of how I decided on Software Engineering and why I believe it's the right fit for me.

The Question Everyone Asks

"What do you want to be when you grow up?" It's a question we hear from childhood, but it becomes weighted when you're 17 and need to choose subjects, then again at 18 when you need to choose a degree.

For me, the path wasn't straight. I explored, questioned, and occasionally panicked about making the "right" choice. This is my story, not advice - your path will be different.

Early Exposure

My first interaction with programming was in high school - basic HTML and CSS. Making a webpage look the way I wanted felt magical. You type some words, it appears on the screen. You change a color, the whole page changes.

It was a small spark. I didn't realize then that it would become a career choice, but looking back, that's where it started.

The Gaming Connection

I've been gaming since I was young. At some point, I started wondering: how do games work? How does a character respond to my button presses? How do developers create entire worlds inside computers?

This curiosity led me to YouTube videos about game development, which introduced me to concepts like game engines, physics engines, and procedural generation. The technical depth behind the games I loved became fascinating.

I realized: software isn't just for big corporations or geniuses in silicon valley. Regular people - people like me - can learn to build things with code.

Why Not Other Fields?

I considered other options seriously before settling on Software Engineering.

Medicine: I respect doctors deeply, but I knew myself well enough to realize that years of medical school and residency, plus the life-or-death pressure, wasn't for me.

Engineering (Civil/Mechanical): My father is an engineer, and I considered following his path. But the physical, hands-on nature of traditional engineering didn't excite me as much as building with code.

Business/Economics: I toyed with the idea of business administration. But it felt too vague - "business" could mean anything. I wanted a specific skill set I could develop.

Arts/Humanities: I enjoy literature and art, but I worried about job prospects and couldn't see myself passionate about those subjects for a lifetime career.

Why Software Engineering Won

It's a transferable skill: Software is everywhere. Healthcare, finance, entertainment, education - every industry needs software. This means job security and variety.

You can build things: Unlike some fields where you're working with abstractions, software lets you create tangible products. You write code, it runs, it works (or you debug it until it does).

Continuous learning: Technology evolves constantly. I love that there's always something new to learn, new problems to solve, new tools to master.

Remote work potential: As a Sri Lankan student, remote work opens up global opportunities. I can work for companies anywhere without relocating.

It combines logic and creativity: Software isn't just math and logic. Building good user experiences requires creativity. It's both sides of my brain working together.

Concerns I Had

I wasn't naive about the challenges. I worried about:

Am I good enough? I'm not a prodigy. I've met students who coded before university. Could I keep up? I realized that comparison is useless - I just need to work hard and progress at my own pace.

Is the field too saturated? Everyone seems to be learning to code. But "saturated" usually means there's demand. Plus, truly good engineers are still rare. Quality matters more than quantity.

Will I burn out? Tech jobs can be demanding. Work-life balance is a real concern. I decided to be mindful of this from the start and prioritize sustainability over maximizing income.

The Decision

In the end, Software Engineering felt right. It aligned with my interests, offered practical skills, and felt like a path where I could build a meaningful career while continuously learning.

Was it the "perfect" choice? Probably not - there's no perfect choice. But it's a choice I can grow into and be happy with.

If you're choosing your path, my advice: consider what you're genuinely curious about, what you're good at, what the world needs, and what will pay the bills. The perfect career sits at the intersection of these. For me, that's Software Engineering.
