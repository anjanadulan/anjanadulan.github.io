My First Year at University: Lessons I Learned

February 5, 2026 6 min read By Anjana Wijerathna

Looking back at my first year studying Software Engineering, there are things I did right and things I wish I'd done differently. Here's what I learned.

The Reality of University vs. Expectations

Before starting university, I had this romanticized view of what Computer Science would be like. I'd seen movies and TV shows depicting programmers as hackers typing frantically on screens, creating revolutionary software in days. The reality was... different.

The first year wasn't about building the next Facebook. It was about understanding how computers actually work at a fundamental level. Binary and hexadecimal, how operating systems manage memory, basic data structures - the unglamorous foundations that make everything else possible.

At first, I was disappointed. I wanted to build cool things, not learn about bitwise operations or memory addresses. But now I understand: you can't build impressive software without understanding how it runs.

What Surprised Me

The math is real: I knew there'd be math, but Discrete Mathematics hit different. Logic, set theory, combinatorics - it's all foundational for algorithms and cryptography. I can't say I love it, but I respect it.

Imposter syndrome starts early: In my first class, someone asked a question I didn't even understand. Everyone else seemed to know things. I spent weeks thinking I didn't belong. Eventually, I realized: everyone felt that way. Everyone was pretending to understand more than they did.

Group projects are... complicated: You learn more about teamwork in group assignments than anywhere else. How to communicate, delegate, handle conflicts, and work with people who have different work ethics. It's stressful but valuable.

Self-learning is expected: Lectures cover concepts, but you'll need to teach yourself implementation. The professor explains what a linked list is; you figure out how to code one at home. University is a guide, not a complete education.

What I Did Wrong (So You Don't Have To)

I didn't ask for help early enough: I struggled with Object-Oriented Programming for weeks before attending office hours. When I finally went, the teaching assistant explained in 10 minutes what I'd been confused about for days. Pride is expensive; office hours are free.

I compared myself to others: Some students came in knowing how to code. They were building websites, contributing to open source, already having GitHub portfolios. I felt behind. But comparison is the thief of joy. I started from zero and I'm progressing. That's what matters.

I didn't build side projects: All my learning was theoretical. I aced the exams but couldn't actually build anything. Projects force you to apply knowledge, fail, debug, and learn. Theory without practice is incomplete.

I neglected my health: Late nights, junk food, no exercise. I gained weight, felt tired constantly, and got sick more often. Your body and mind are connected. Taking care of physical health improves mental performance.

What I Did Right

I started coding daily: Even just 30 minutes of coding practice every day made a difference. Consistency beats intensity. I built small projects, did LeetCode problems, followed tutorials - anything to keep coding.

I joined communities: Discord servers for developers, study groups on campus, online forums. Having people to ask questions, share struggles, and celebrate wins made the journey less lonely.

I started my portfolio early: Even with basic projects, I documented my learning on GitHub. It forced me to think about presenting my work and built habits I'll need for job hunting later.

I gave myself grace: Some weeks were terrible. I failed assignments, did poorly on exams, felt overwhelmed. But I didn't quit. I acknowledged the setback and moved forward. Progress isn't linear.

Advice for New CS Students

Learn the fundamentals well: Don't rush to frameworks and libraries before understanding basics. HTML before React. Basic SQL before complex ORMs. The fundamentals transfer; frameworks come and go.

Build projects, not just for grades: Course projects are required. Personal projects are chosen. The personal ones are where you truly learn because there's no rubric, no instructions - just problems to solve.

Read documentation: Official documentation is often the best resource. It might be dense, but it's accurate and comprehensive. Learn to parse technical writing.

Get comfortable with being uncomfortable: Programming means constantly facing problems you don't know how to solve. That's not a bug; it's the job. Embrace the discomfort and develop persistence.

Take breaks: Staring at code for hours leads to diminishing returns. Step away, sleep on problems, let your subconscious work. I've solved more bugs on walks than at my desk.

Looking Forward

My first year wasn't what I expected, but it was what I needed. I emerged with a stronger foundation, realistic expectations, and a community of fellow travelers.

Second year is about going deeper: more complex algorithms, bigger projects, specialization paths. I'm nervous and excited in equal measure.

If you're starting university for CS or a related field: enjoy the journey. It's hard, sometimes frustrating, and absolutely worth it.
